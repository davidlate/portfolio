This section offers some more insight into the various engineering skills I've built up over the years. I look forward to continuing to learn and grow in my next opportunity.

Solidworks Skills

  1. CAD Modelling and Part Design

    I have 3+ years experience using Solidworks for CAD modelling, and can model at a professional level, including but not limited to part and mult-part assembly design and sheet metal.

    In creating designs I pay heavy attention to tolerance stackups between assembly parts, which can often become complicated when factoring in expected thermal expansion.

    In selecting materials I've considered cost, manufacturability, dimensional stability, rigidity, strength, density, UV resistance, temperature resistance, thermal expansion, thermal conductivity, visual appearance, porosity and susceptibity to expansion when wet, corrosion resistance, and chemical compatibility with other materials in the assembly.

  2. Drawings using GD&T

    I am proficient using GD&T following ASME Y14.5, and have primarily used it for flatness, parallelism, press-fits, and true positioning of holes.

    It's useful for using alignment hardware to create precise assembly fits and for ensuring proper fluid sealing and heat transfer without "overspecifying" tolerances.

    I've found the SAE GD&T Pocket Guide is a very useful reference - particularly for understand datum assignments and the meanings behind tolerance callouts (for example: how a ⌀.014" true position callout can end up being as tight as +/-.005", and how to understand MMC callouts), although many of the examples in the book would only be used in very specific applications.

    In most all cases, it's best to talk with the vendor to find out their preference, oftentimes I have been surprised with the capabilities they've offered as being easy, such as 5-axis machining, as technology has advanced over the years.
    The Machinery's Handbook also has some nice examples of GD&T applications.

  3. FEA Analysis

    I've used Solidworks for static FEA analyses in both thermal and structural applications. From testing I've learned that seemingly simple fixturing assumptions can have significant effects on the result which may or may not be correct.

    I've gone through and physically tested designs influenced by the FEA and been able to see when those assumptions were accurate and when they needed to be adjusted.

Design for Manufacturing

  1. CNC Machining

    Through my work experiences I've designed a large number of parts to be machined by both CNC mills and lathes.

    These have been made by quick-turn suppliers, local vendors, myself, and occasionally other in-house employees. I have a solid understanding of the techniques required, including but not limited to: fixturability, leaving in tool radii for endmills, reasonable tolerances, and keeping an eye on acceptable l/d ratios. I've incorporated techniques such as reaming and press-fit alignment hardware.

    I've learned inspecting fabricated parts in-house upon arrival is very important, especially since many vendors will only fully inspect the first article if not otherwise specified.

  2. Sheet Metal

    I've designed and tested parts using sheet metal. The main considerations I follow are to keep an eye on minimum bend radii, and the tolerancing stackups between holes and other features. K-factors are one way to measure bend radii, though some suppliers will have a fixed recommendation that works with their tools, and others will specify the radius as a multiple of material thickness.

    Generally, tolerancing between parts on the same piece can be quite good, but features with bends between them can be off by as much as +/- .030", necessitating workarounds such as using slots instead of holes.

    Sheet metal is also nice because of ease of incorporating PEM inserts

  3. Injection Molding

    I've become familiar with the principles of injection molding design, but due to the high cost of soft- and hard-tooling I have not created a design to be injection molded yet.

    The main considerations I'm aware of are to maintain a consistent nominal wall thickness throughout the entire part, incorporate features to allow for endmill radii when fabricating the molds, incorporating a 3-4° draft angle on all vertical faces, and being mindful of where the injection points may be so that the plastic doesn't cool down before fully filling the mold.

  4. Additive Manufacturing / 3D Printing Metal & Plastic

    I've prototyped extensively using resin SLA 3D printers, which are very nice for complex geometries but struggle with respect to dimensional stability and print times.

    In particular they can have a tendency to warp toward the "supported" side while curing and they struggle with internal cavities where fluid is unable to drain during printing. Care must be taken to orient the most critical features correctly on the build platform to maximize chances for a successful part.

    I've also used 3D printed metal for rapid prototyping; this enables quick production of very complex geometries but requires certain considerations to be used effectively. In particular, it leaves a very rough surface finish which often requires post-machining, but the rough surfaces don't provide good reference surfaces to fixture and align the post-machining operation.

    Additionally, there can be voids hidden in the material, the complex geometries make complete inspection difficult, and there may be leftover un-sintered powder hidden in cavities. Additionally, there are fewer resources available for the material properties of 3D printed metal - including strength, thermal conductivity, and material compatibility; and the properties that are available must be taken with a grain of salt as the 3D printed materials are anisotropic.

Microcontrollers and Automation

  1. Raspberry Pi and BeagleBone

    I've been experimenting with small Single-Board computers like the Raspberry Pi and BeagleBone since sophomore year in college. A BeagleBone is similar to a Raspberry Pi, but is readily available and doesn't suffer from the Pi's supply chain issues. The catch is that BeagleBones are much slower, often don't include Wi-Fi functionality, and mainly work through just a command-line interface (the GUI is not great, in my opinion). Some things I have done with these devices include:

    • Reading and recording data from multiple sensors, and using data to automatically control hardware, with the help of a LabJack device (detailed further in following sections).

    • Connecting and reading data over SSH. I like the screen application that maintains a single SSH session even if the original connection is disconnected so long as the Pi remains on, and can be configured to run automatically on startup.

    • Driving servos and stepper motors using PWM hardware pins as part of my college capstone.

    • Driving gear motors and recording their position using rotary encoders.

    • Running python scripts on startup using crontab, and incorporating simple linux scripts to automatically mount and unmount USB drives and SD cards on startup or upon a button push.

    • Re-soldering the mini-USB socket back onto a Raspberry Pi.

    In my opinion, these computers are limited because they don't use a Real-Time Operating System, and as such your programs may be somewhat subject to the whims of the Linux operating system, interrupting their timing. This is usually not an issue, but can make it difficult to run programs on startup and maintain precise fixed sampling intervals without external hardware to help. In my opinion most setups function much better when the brunt of the sampling is dependent on a microcontroller such as an Arduino or a DAQ such as a LabJack, with the Raspberry Pi being used to record the data and access the internet.


    In my opinion these devices would hugely benefit from a Real-Time Clock addition such as this, which I look forward to trying.

Testing and Sensor Integration

  1. Sensor Uncertainty

    One of the most important yet frequently overlooked aspects of sensor selection is uncertainty analysis, particularly using the error propagation formula. As an example, it can be easy to purchase thermocouples to measure sensible heat rise in a fluid and assume that the nominal +/- 1° accuracy seems good enough - then do the math and realize that the uncertainty is much greater than the expected result!
    Similarly, some instruments such as the clamp ammeters can actually be very accurate for some applications, which wouldn't be apparent without working out how the combination of uncertainties affects the measured variable.


    In any test setup, it's best for every measured variable to be acquired using sensors that have manufacturer's accuracy specifications, and that the expected root-sum-square uncertainty is sufficiently less than the expected measurement quantity.

  3. Temperature Measurement

    • Grounded or Ungrounded Thermocouples?

      As a very brief summary, "grounded" thermocouples are electrically bonded to the body of the device they're enclosed in, while "ungrounded" thermocouples are electrically "insulated" from the chassis.

      Generally, ungrounded thermocouples are used when measuring conductive media or when installed with other thermocouples being read by the same device into a common conductive chassis, such as steel piping.

      Grounded thermocouples are used when measuring non-conductive media such as air. When multiple thermocouples are electrically joined in more than one place, their signals can interfere with each other in a phenomenon called a "ground loop" which will give incorrect readings.


      The "insulated" is in italics because, in some cases, there is still enough electrical conductivity between the measuring element and the ungrounded thermocouple chassis to cause problems. This comes into play because some multi-channel thermocouple readers use a common electrical ground plane for all channels, instead of isolating all of the channels.

      If the ungrounded thermocouples do not provide enough insulation, this common ground plane can act as the second connection point between thermocouples, and cause the ground loop errors. The best way to avoid this error is to measure the electrical resistance between the thermocouple leads and the thermocouple chassis, and the resistance between each channel on the thermocouple reader being used.

Fluid Transport and Plumbing

  1. Fittings

    I've used a number of different fitting types and sizes for creating setups for plumbing, including water and air. Here are some other connection types I've used:

    • Swagelok / Compression Fittings

      My preferred connection type is the Swagelok compression connection for its re-usability, durability, and ability to be re-clocked easily, unlike threaded connections like NPT. However, the threads are prone to galling if the connection is over-torqued, and although they are stainless steel, they aren't fully immune to corrosion. Not to mention they can be fantastically expensive compared to other options.

    • Push-Connect & Universal Thread

      Push-connects are cheap, and great to have around for simple prototyping runs as they're dead-simple to use and generally tend to do the job well for non-critical applications. Just be sure to triple-check they're fully seated before applying any pressure.

      The Universal Thread adapters are really nice, too. The one thread interfaces with both NPT and BSPP threads and seals with a gasket. The gasket usually lasts for just one installation, but it's usually enough to get the job done in a pinch and they're very affordable.

    • NPT

      NPT threads are one of the most common sealing threads on the market today. Pro's include ease-of-use, simplicity, and the confidence of using a tried-and-true sealing solution. Cons include difficulty of installation, particularly in hard-to-reach areas, difficulty "clocking" the installed fitting, the somewhat destructive nature of the interference fit when using fittings made of soft material such as brass, and the risk of Teflon tape or pipe dope breaking off into the fluid flow and fouling a part of the system downstream.


      Advice varies from person to person on the necessity of Teflon tape and/or pipe dope. The general consensus I've come upon is that Teflon tape is good for metal-to-metal connections, but pipe dope should be used if either of the connecting materials are plastic. Though, whenever the application requirements allow, I'll use Teflon tape on plastic parts as well to avoid the mess of pipe dope. Others will suggest to use both Teflon tape and pipe dope on all fittings to further prevent leaks.


      When joining plastic and metal fittings with interference threads, it's important to have the external thread be of a stronger material than the internal thread (i.e. insert a plastic fitting into a metal one, not the other way around) to prevent cracking. Additionally, when expensive equipment arrives with integral plastic NPT adapters, I'll usually add a cheap brass fitting before plumbing it into the system to ensure that any thread damage happens to the cheap fitting, and not the connection on the expensive equipment.

    • BSPP / G threads

      BSPP threads are straight pipe threads that seal with a gasket. Though they don't provide clockability like Swagelok fittings, they make an excellent and consistent seal. I'm a big fan of this connection type, especially due to the price point. The main consideration with these fittings is ensuring that there is a suitable flat surface for the o-ring or gasket to seal to.

  2. Valves

    • Globe, Ball, & Needle Valves

      The main two features I for in valve selection is the Cv, which determines the head losses and maximum flow rate through the valve; the valve size, which determines the velocity through the valve and can impact whether cavitation occurs; and the number of turns to close.

      The number of turns to close is an important parameter because it influences the amount of control precision the valve will have, and conversely how quickly it can be actuated. A globe valve with a 1/4 turn-to-close would make a poor flow control valve for testing, just as an 8 turn-to-close needle valve would make for a poor emergency shutoff valve.

  2. CNC Machining

    I have experience using Solidworks CAM and Fusion 360 to create small teste fixtures and prototypes out of plastic, aluminum, and copper. I like the workflow of Solidworks CAM, but Fusion 360 has the capability to create trochoidal toolpaths which, in my experience greatly improves machining times by using chip thinning. These toolpaths, also referred to as "High Speed Machining", increase fab speed and tool life by using a much deeper axial depth-of-cut paired with a much shallower radial depth-of-cut (5-10% instead of 30-40%) and greatly increased feedrates.
